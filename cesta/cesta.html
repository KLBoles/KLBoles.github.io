Course Access: Analytic Strategy

  • One logit for each AP® course \[\frac{P_i}{1-P_i} = exp(\beta_{0} + \beta_{1}City_{i} + \beta_{2}Rural_{i} + \beta_{3}Town_{i} + \beta_{4}log(SES)_{i} + \\ \beta_{5}\sqrt{Enrollment}\;_{i} + \beta_{6}log(Prop\;Black)_{i} + \beta_{7}log(Prop\;Latinx)_{i} + \beta_{8}log(Prop\;Asian)_{i} + \alpha_{i}+\epsilon_{i})\]
    • \(P_{i}\)= Probability of offering course at school i
    • \(City_{i},Rural_{i},Town_{i}\)= NCES locale indicator variables
    • \(SES_{i}\)= Composite SES variable ranging from 0 - 999
    • \(Enrollment_{i}\)= Student school population
    • \(Prop\;Black_{i},Prop\;Latinx_{i},Prop\;Asian_{i}\)= Proportion of enrollment Black, Latinx, Asian
    • \(\alpha_{i}\)= state fixed-effects

The logit models I just showed are aspatial - meaning they didn’t incorporate where they schools were in space. W/O spatial data best we can do is categorical variables like locale and the state fixed effects.

With spatial data and spatial data science, we acknowledge that variables change across space - like across schools (points) and across counties(polygons)

First Law of Geography * Everything is related but closer things are more related than farther things

We look for this before we run a spatial model…

Teacher Learning: Context

  • Teachers learn from four spheres — interpersonal, institutional, community, and policy1

  • Learning partners and distributed resources2 comprise teachers’ learning ecologies:

the accessed set of contexts, comprised of configurations of activities, material resources and relationships, found in co-located physical or virtual spaces that provide opportunities for learning (Barron, 2004, p.6)3

  1. Zavelevsky, E., & Lishchinsky, O. S. (2020). An ecological perspective of teacher retention: An emergent model. Teaching and Teacher Education, 88, 1-11.

  2. Pea, R. (1993). Practices of distributed intelligence and designed for education. In G. Salomon (Ed.), Distributed cognitions: Psychological and educational considerations (pp. 47-87). Cambridge, UK: Cambridge University Press.

  3. Barron, B. (2004). Learning ecologies for technological fluency: Gender and experience differences. Journal of Educational Computing Research, 31 (1), 1-36.
